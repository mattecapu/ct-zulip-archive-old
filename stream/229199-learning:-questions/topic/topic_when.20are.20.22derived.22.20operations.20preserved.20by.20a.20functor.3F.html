Topic: when are "derived" operations preserved by a functor?

David Egolf (Jun 01 2023 at 16:59):

A functor F:C→DF: C \to DF:C→D preserves the operation ∘\circ∘ of composition in the sense that F(f∘g)=F(f)∘F(g)F(f \circ g) = F(f) \circ F(g)F(f∘g)=F(f)∘F(g). It is possible to define a new two-argument operation in terms of ∘\circ∘, and I'm curious when such an operation will also be preserved by the functor.

For example, let f∗g=f∘g∘ff*g = f \circ g \circ ff∗g=f∘g∘f. When fff and ggg are such that f∗gf*gf∗g is defined, then F(f∗g)=F(f∘g∘f)=F(f)∘F(g)∘F(f)=F(f)∗F(g)F(f * g) = F(f \circ g \circ f) = F(f) \circ F(g) \circ F(f) = F(f)*F(g)F(f∗g)=F(f∘g∘f)=F(f)∘F(g)∘F(f)=F(f)∗F(g). In this case, the operation ∗*∗ is preserved by FFF. As a second example, let f□g=f∘f∘g∘gf \square g = f \circ f \circ g \circ gf□g=f∘f∘g∘g. When f□gf \square gf□g is defined, then F(f□g)=F(f∘f∘g∘g)=F(f)∘F(f)∘F(g)∘F(g)=F(f)□F(g)F(f \square g) = F(f \circ f \circ g \circ g) = F(f) \circ F(f) \circ F(g) \circ F(g) = F(f) \square F(g)F(f□g)=F(f∘f∘g∘g)=F(f)∘F(f)∘F(g)∘F(g)=F(f)□F(g). I'm curious if this always works. However, I'm not sure how to tackle this question. Once I understand the case above, I'm also interested in the case where FFF is a map that preserves multiple operations. For example, if FFF is a homorphism between Boolean algebras then F(a∧b)=F(a)∧F(b)F(a \land b) = F(a) \land F(b)F(a∧b)=F(a)∧F(b) and F(a∨b)=F(a)∨F(b)F(a \lor b) = F(a) \lor F(b)F(a∨b)=F(a)∨F(b). Then will FFF preserve new two-argument operations defined in terms of ∧\land∧ and ∨\lor∨?

Mike Shulman (Jun 01 2023 at 17:11):

In general the answer is yes, and one way to make it precise is with universal-algebraic tools such as "operads" and "Lawvere theories". For instance, the Lawvere theory of Boolean algebras is a category B\mathbf{B}B with finite products generated "freely" by one object XXX that has the structure of an internal Boolean algebra. Then the morphisms Xn→XX^n \to XXn→X in this category are precisely the "nnn-ary operations that can be defined in terms of the defining Boolean algebra operations". Moreover, freeness of B\mathbf{B}B means that to give an actual Boolean algebra is equivalent to giving a finite-product-preserving functor B→Set\mathbf{B} \to \rm SetB→Set, and similarly to give a Boolean algebra homomorphism is equivalent to giving a natural transformation between such functors. Naturality of such a transformation then tells you that any such homomorphism automatically commutes with any of the "definable operations". The case of categories is similar, except that you need something fancier instead of a finite-product category.
