categories, opfibrations, and natural transformations

Matteo Capucci (he/him) (Mar 21 2021 at 09:20):

So, the first obstacle, as Mike points out, is that there's no explicit way to see whether two pseudofunctors are composable, since you don't have the total category of the second to check whether it matches the first's base.
Working one dimension down, when you have functors F:X→SetF : X \to SetF:X→Set, G:Y→SetG : Y \to SetG:Y→Set with X,YX, YX,Y sets, you can define their composition as the ⊲\lhd⊲ (= composition of functors) of their associated polynomial functors. As we discussed yesterday, Bruno, this means that you don't assume the total space of one coincides with the base of the other, you simply assume there's some map between the two and index over the collection of all these maps. Indeed, F⊲FF \lhd FF⊲F gives you:

∑x∈XYF(x)∋φ↦∑z∈F(x)G(φ(z))\sum_{x \in X} Y^{F(x)} \ni \varphi \mapsto \sum_{z \in F(x)} G(\varphi(z))x∈X∑​YF(x)∋φ↦z∈F(x)∑​G(φ(z))

Specifying a mapping (x:X)→Y→F(x)(x : X) \to Y \to F(x)(x:X)→Y→F(x) allows you to collapse this into a functor X→SetX \to SetX→Set. Indeed, if you know in advance that Y=∑x∈XF(x)Y = \sum_{x \in X} F(x)Y=∑x∈X​F(x), then there's a canonical way to do so, namely with the map x↦ιxx \mapsto \iota_xx↦ιx​, where ιx\iota_xιx​ is the inclusion of F(x)F(x)F(x) into YYY.
I suspect the above can be easily generalized to the next dimension by noting that the Grothendieck construction is an oplax colimit, hence you get 'inclusions' of fibers into it. The rest stays pretty much the same, I guess.

That said, this is all very painful.

Joe Moeller (Mar 23 2021 at 21:06):

The idea is that when you start with a pseudofunctor F ⁣:Bop→CatF \colon B^{op} \to \mathsf{Cat}F:Bop→Cat, and you do the Grothendieck construction to get a category ∫F\int F∫F, you have forgotten something about the original piece of data: you forgot that F(b)F(b)F(b) and F(b′)F(b')F(b′) where separate categories. There is a straightforward functor ∫F→B\int F \to B∫F→B which does remember that data.

Joe Moeller (Mar 23 2021 at 21:06):

But not all functors arise this way. You could view fibrations as a characterization of precisely which functors you get this way.

Joe Moeller (Mar 23 2021 at 21:12):

Specifically, the lifting property tells you that the maps in ∫F\int F∫F which did not only arise as the maps in the categories F(b)F(b)F(b), but as one which actually has stuff coming from the nontrivial functors of the form F(f) ⁣:F(b)→F(b′)F(f) \colon F(b) \to F(b')F(f):F(b)→F(b′) in them, can be sorta "straightened back out" into functors between the "fibers".
