I was reading in "Category Theory In Context" (by Riehl) and was struck by this statement on page 3:

...the algebra of morphisms determines the category...

This is interesting to consider in the context of groups.
A single group GGG, viewed as a category, is specified in this way as follows, I think:

  • GGG is a category with a single object (or, equivalently, having a single identity morphism ididid)
  • For any morphism fff in GGG, there exists a morphism ggg so fg=gf=idfg = gf = idfg=gf=id
  • For any two morphisms, fff and hhh in GGG, we know what their composite morphism is, fhfhfh
    So, the algebra of morphisms specifies the group.

However, it seems that we usually do things a bit differently when defining the category Grp\mathsf{Grp}Grp having groups as its objects and group homomorphisms as its morphisms. This specification of the category goes something like this:
-The objects are all the groups
-A morphism fff from GGG to HHH must satisfy f(a)f(b)=f(ab)f(a)f(b) = f(ab)f(a)f(b)=f(ab) for all a,b∈Ga,b \in Ga,b∈G, where f(a),f(b),f(ab)∈Hf(a), f(b), f(ab) \in Hf(a),f(b),f(ab)∈H. In addition, we must have f(idG)=idHf(id_G) = id_Hf(idG​)=idH​.
This description of Grp\mathsf{Grp}Grp "mixes levels", in that it uses data about composition of morphisms in the categories one sees when "zooming in" on individual groups. It does not directly specify the algebra of composition of morphisms in Grp\mathsf{Grp}Grp. As a result, when working in Grp\mathsf{Grp}Grp, one often considers elements inside objects - instead of focusing entirely on morphisms between objects.

How might one go about specifying Grp\mathsf{Grp}Grp by directly describing the algebra of morphisms, without using the auxiliary concept of elements internal to the obejcts?
(My initial idea is to start by considering the algebra of morphisms about special universal objects and try to build up things from there).

Nathanael Arkor (Dec 07 2021 at 18:13):

David Egolf said:

How might one go about specifying Grp\mathsf{Grp}Grp by directly describing the algebra of morphisms, without using the auxiliary concept of elements internal to the obejcts?

This is essentially how [[Lawvere theories]] work: there is a Lawvere theory G\mathbb GG of groups, and the category of groups is given by the category of finite-product-preserving functors from G\mathbb GG into Set\mathbf{Set}Set. All the elementwise structure is encapsulated in the category of sets, distinct from the pure algebraic structure of groups.

John Baez (Dec 07 2021 at 18:33):

But in this approach a group is still a set equipped with extra structure. You can't instantly read off the morphisms in Grp\mathsf{Grp}Grp from looking at the Lawvere theory G\mathbb{G}G. You have to determine the models of G\mathbb{G}G, which are sets equipped with operations in G\mathbb{G}G (namely groups), and then determine the morphisms between these models, which are functions preserving these operations (namely group homomorphisms).

Lawvere theories are great, but we don't want David to be fooled into thinking they're a trick for "describing the algebra of morphisms [in Grp\mathsf{Grp}Grp], without using the auxiliary concept of elements internal to the objects".

John Baez (Dec 07 2021 at 18:35):

What they are is a trick for describing all the operations that any group has, and the laws they obey, without referring to any particular group or its elements.

I am beginning to think that some statements about individual groups are just best to state in terms of elements, even if they can be instead stated in terms of morphisms to and from the group. I did notice (I think) that the compositional structure of a group can be detected by morphisms from the free group on two elements, which was interesting. If we wish to know what the result is from composing aaa with bbb in a group GGG, we can consider the morphism (a,b)(a,b)(a,b) from the free group on two elements - which sends the first generator to aaa and the second to bbb. If we call the two generators g1g_1g1​ and g2g_2g2​, then (a,b)(g1g2)=(a,b)(g1)(a,b)(g2)=ab(a,b)(g_1 g_2) = (a,b)(g_1) (a,b)(g_2) = ab(a,b)(g1​g2​)=(a,b)(g1​)(a,b)(g2​)=ab. So, if we have a description of terms of elements of this morphism, we can figure out the internal structure of GGG in terms of elements. It can also be interesting to look at the kernel of (a,b)(a,b)(a,b), which tells us about the non-free relationships that hold between aaa and bbb. However, I don't think this is really helpful in providing a different perspective on proving things about elements in a group that are required to hold for all groups (such as cancellation).

David Egolf (Dec 13 2021 at 23:53):

Earlier in this thread, I was talking about how I wanted to relate statements in an introductory abstract algebra book to corresponding categorical statements. One such statement is that left-cancellation holds in a group: in a group GGG, we have ab=ac  ⟹  b=cab = ac \implies b=cab=ac⟹b=c.

This statement can be proved in categorical language, but the way I was doing this did not seem that interesting. I was modelling the group GGG as a category with a single object where each morphism is an isomorphism. The process of proof ends up being very similar to that in the introductory book.

However, I was recently learning about opposite categories and this gave me an idea for how to make the categorical exploration of these theorems a little more interesting. The idea is to follow this process:

  1. Consider some statement SSS that we want to show holds for a group GGG
  2. "Lift" that statement to a statement about an arbitrary category CCC
  3. Prove that statement in this more general setting, so we learn the statement holds for any category
  4. Note that the statement also holds for the opposite category CopC^{op}Cop
  5. Translate the statement in CopC^{op}Cop back to a statement in CCC to obtain a "dual" theorem in CCC
  6. If possible, "lower" this statement to obtain a dual theorem about the group GGG
    If this process works, we can get a dual theorem for our original theorem about groups that we started with.

Here is how the process would look for the left cancellation statement above:

  1. Our statement SSS is "in a group GGG, we have ab=ac  ⟹  b=cab = ac \implies b=cab=ac⟹b=c"
  2. Lifting the statement yields: In a category CCC, we have ab=ac  ⟹  b=cab=ac \implies b =cab=ac⟹b=c for isomorphisms a,b,ca,b,ca,b,c.
  3. Composing on the left with a−1a^{-1}a−1 shows that ab=ac  ⟹  b=cab=ac \implies b=cab=ac⟹b=c.
  4. This statement holds in any category, and so in particular it holds in the opposite category: ab=ac  ⟹  b=cab=ac \implies b =cab=ac⟹b=c for isomorphisms a,b,ca,b,ca,b,c in CopC^{op}Cop.
  5. Translating the statement of the theorem in the opposite category back to CCC: ba=ca  ⟹  b=cba=ca \implies b =cba=ca⟹b=c for isomorphisms a,b,ca,b,ca,b,c. This statement I think still holds for any category. (I'm still figuring out how opposite categories work, so hopefully this is correct).
  6. In particular, the statement holds for GGG viewed as a category. We get: "In a group GGG, we have ba=ca  ⟹  b=cba=ca \implies b=cba=ca⟹b=c".
    If this process was done correctly, it would seem that right-cancellation in a group is "dual" to left-cancellation!
    This seems really neat, because it gives us a second theorem from a starting theorem.

Morgan Rogers (he/him) (Dec 14 2021 at 07:08):

Re using duality to get theorems for free: this two for the price of one observation is used all over the place. You might have heard that left adjoints preserve colimits; the dual result is that right adjoints preserve limits, and we get this for free!
Some definitions of specific classes of category are self-dual, which allows us to perform similar deductions. For example, the definition of abelian category is self-dual, which means that any result one can prove about abelian categories is also true for opposites of abelian categories. This becomes a lot more interesting when you consider that the typical examples of abelian categories (categories of modules over commutative rings, say) are far from being equivalent to their opposites.
