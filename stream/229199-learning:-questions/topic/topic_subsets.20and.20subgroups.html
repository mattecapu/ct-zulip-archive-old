Tobias Schmude (Aug 21 2022 at 19:15):

Unfortunately this does not work! But seeing why is quite enlightening. The concept you're after is called a [[subobject classifier]], and it exists in the category Set\mathrm{Set}Set, but not in Grp\mathrm{Grp}Grp. Let's see why that is the case:

In full generality, a subobject classifier is an object Ω\OmegaΩ of a category C\mathcal{C}C (which has to have pullbacks of monomorphisms) such that subobjects of any object correspond to morphisms into Ω\OmegaΩ (e.g. Ω={0,1}\Omega = \{0, 1\}Ω={0,1} in Set\mathrm{Set}Set). To phrase it categorically:
We have two presheaves on C\mathcal{C}C. Firstly there is Sub\mathrm{Sub}Sub, defined on objects by taking the set of subobjects and on morphisms by pullback (that's why we need them). Secondly there is C(−,Ω)\mathcal{C}(-, \Omega)C(−,Ω), defined on objects by taking the set of morphisms into Ω\OmegaΩ and on morphisms by composition. We demand these to be naturally isomorphic, i.e. Sub\mathrm{Sub}Sub is represented by Ω\OmegaΩ.

This gives us a necessary criterion for the existence of a subobject classifier: representable presheaves preserve limits! Due to contravariance this amounts to mapping colimits in C\mathcal{C}C to limits in Set\mathrm{Set}Set.
Let's concentrate on the special case of coproducts: this says that for objects C,DC, DC,D, subobjects of the coproduct C∪DC \cup DC∪D have to be in bijection with pairs of subobjects of CCC and DDD.

In Set\mathrm{Set}Set this is clear: a subset S⊆C∪DS \subseteq C \cup DS⊆C∪D is equivalently given by the pair of subsets S∩C⊆CS \cap C \subseteq CS∩C⊆C and S∩D⊆DS \cap D \subseteq DS∩D⊆D.

In the category Grp\mathrm{Grp}Grp this is not the case though. Take for example finite groups GGG and HHH with nontrivial involutions g∈G,h∈Hg \in G, h \in Hg∈G,h∈H. Then there are only finitely many pairs of subgroups, but the coproduct ("free product" in group theorists' terms) contains infinitely many distinct involutions

(g),(h),(g,h,g),(h,g,h),(g,h,g,h,g),(h,g,h,g,h),…(g), (h), (g, h, g), (h, g, h), (g, h, g, h, g), (h, g, h, g, h), \dots(g),(h),(g,h,g),(h,g,h),(g,h,g,h,g),(h,g,h,g,h),…

which accordingly generate infinitely many distinct subgroups.

Tobias Schmude (Aug 21 2022 at 20:32):

Thanks! The nlab mentions another, more conceptual, way to see that Grp\mathrm{Grp}Grp does not have a subobject classifier:
First off, the trivial group is a zero object (both initial and terminal).
In a category with zero object, any subobject classified by a hypothetical subobject classifier turns out to be a kernel of its classifying map (see Prop 4.1 on the nlab).
This is a problem since not every subgroup is a kernel ("normal" subgroup).

That makes sense. In categories with a subobject classifier, to my understanding, we can associate the subobjects of an object AAA with the maps from it to Ω\OmegaΩ. So - in this special case, we don't need all the morphisms from AAA (and how they can be converted to others morphisms from AAA by morphisms between other objects) - we just need those to a single object.
So, on one extreme the Yoneda Lemma tells us that maps to all other objects from AAA (and how they can be converted to one another via morphisms between other objects) is enough to tell us about the subobjects of AAA. And on the other extreme in some cases (when we have a subobject classifier) the maps to just one of the objects from AAA is enough to talk about subobjects of AAA.

Maybe there is a whole ascending ladder of complexity of categories in between these two cases, though! (Where knowing everything about the morphisms out of AAA to two, or three, or n special objects is enough to determine the subobjects of AAA).

An [[extensive category]] is a category with coproducts and equivalences of slice categories C/(A+B)≃C/A×C/B\mathcal{C}/(A+B) \simeq \mathcal{C}/A \times \mathcal{C}/BC/(A+B)≃C/A×C/B. Since Sub(A){\rm Sub}(A)Sub(A) is the poset of subterminal objects in C/A\mathcal{C}/AC/A, any extensive category satisfies Sub(A+B)≅Sub(A)×Sub(B){\rm Sub}(A+B) \cong {\rm Sub}(A)\times {\rm Sub}(B)Sub(A+B)≅Sub(A)×Sub(B). But there are plenty of extensive categories without a subobject classifier, including (as mentioned) Cat\rm CatCat and Top\rm TopTop.

Ralph Sarkis said:

Evident follow-up question: is there a "normal subgroup classifier" in Grp\mathbf{Grp}Grp?

There isn't, here's the smallest possible counterexample in all glory:
Let's take G=H=Z2G = H = \mathbb{Z}_2G=H=Z2​, and rename the 1 from each cofactor to aaa and bbb respectively. Then the coproduct consists of the words of alternating a's and b's, where pairs of a's and b's cancel. What does conjugation do? If the length of the word is even, conjugation with either aaa or bbb reverses (= inverts) the word, so there's nothing new to gain. This means that for each natural number nnn we have normal subgroups consisting of the words of length divisible by 2n2n2n.
Just for "fun": on a word with length 2n+12n+12n+1, conjugation produces either the word with length 2(n+1)+12(n+1)+12(n+1)+1 starting with the other letter, or the word with length 2(n−1)+12(n-1)+12(n−1)+1 starting with the other letter in case n≥1n \geq 1n≥1. So immediately any normal subgroup containing a word of odd length contains the words of length 4n+14n+14n+1 starting with either aaa or with bbb, as well as the words of length 4n+34n+34n+3 starting with the other. By conjugation and multiplication it also contains all words of length divisible by 4. Multiplication doesn't lead out of those, so we get two further normal subgroups consisting of the words of length divisible by 4 as well as either of the two families of words of odd length.
If we further assume that it contains a word of length 4n+24n+24n+2 or a word from the other family of words of odd length, we get all words, so that's it.
