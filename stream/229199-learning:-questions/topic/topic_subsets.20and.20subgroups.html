David Egolf (Aug 21 2022 at 16:16):

I learned recently that the subsets of a set SSS are in 1-1 correspondence with the maps 2S2^S2S, namely those from SSS to 222, where 222 is some set with two elements, say {1,0}\{1,0\}{1,0}. Then the subset T⊆ST \subseteq ST⊆S is specified by sending its elements to 1 and all the other elements of SSS to 0.

I was wondering if we can do this with groups, so as to get some set that corresponds to all the subgroups of a given group GGG. Let 222 now be a group with two elements {0,1}\{0,1\}{0,1}. There are two subgroups of 222: the one associated with the identity element, and the one associated with all of 222. A group homomorphism f:G→2f: G \to 2f:G→2 might be able to, intuitively, send all subgroups except one to the identity element subgroup (and the remaining one of interest to all of 222). However, a group homomorphism is required to preserve some structure, and so I'm worried that such a group homomorphism won't necessarily exist. As one example, it seems like we can't specify the identity element subgroup by mapping it to all of 222 - it has to get mapped to the identity element subgroup of 222. So this seems like it isn't working out.

(In a little more detail, if we have a surjective group homomorphism fff from GGG to 2, then G/ker⁡(f)≅2G/\ker(f) \cong 2G/ker(f)≅2. That's a pretty restrictive condition on the structure of GGG, I think.)

Is there some nice concise way to talk about all the subgroups of a group GGG, in terms of maps from GGG?

Tobias Schmude (Aug 21 2022 at 19:15):

Unfortunately this does not work! But seeing why is quite enlightening. The concept you're after is called a [[subobject classifier]], and it exists in the category Set\mathrm{Set}Set, but not in Grp\mathrm{Grp}Grp. Let's see why that is the case:

In full generality, a subobject classifier is an object Ω\OmegaΩ of a category C\mathcal{C}C (which has to have pullbacks of monomorphisms) such that subobjects of any object correspond to morphisms into Ω\OmegaΩ (e.g. Ω={0,1}\Omega = \{0, 1\}Ω={0,1} in Set\mathrm{Set}Set). To phrase it categorically:
We have two presheaves on C\mathcal{C}C. Firstly there is Sub\mathrm{Sub}Sub, defined on objects by taking the set of subobjects and on morphisms by pullback (that's why we need them). Secondly there is C(−,Ω)\mathcal{C}(-, \Omega)C(−,Ω), defined on objects by taking the set of morphisms into Ω\OmegaΩ and on morphisms by composition. We demand these to be naturally isomorphic, i.e. Sub\mathrm{Sub}Sub is represented by Ω\OmegaΩ.

This gives us a necessary criterion for the existence of a subobject classifier: representable presheaves preserve limits! Due to contravariance this amounts to mapping colimits in C\mathcal{C}C to limits in Set\mathrm{Set}Set.
Let's concentrate on the special case of coproducts: this says that for objects C,DC, DC,D, subobjects of the coproduct C∪DC \cup DC∪D have to be in bijection with pairs of subobjects of CCC and DDD.

In Set\mathrm{Set}Set this is clear: a subset S⊆C∪DS \subseteq C \cup DS⊆C∪D is equivalently given by the pair of subsets S∩C⊆CS \cap C \subseteq CS∩C⊆C and S∩D⊆DS \cap D \subseteq DS∩D⊆D.

In the category Grp\mathrm{Grp}Grp this is not the case though. Take for example finite groups GGG and HHH with nontrivial involutions g∈G,h∈Hg \in G, h \in Hg∈G,h∈H. Then there are only finitely many pairs of subgroups, but the coproduct ("free product" in group theorists' terms) contains infinitely many distinct involutions

(g),(h),(g,h,g),(h,g,h),(g,h,g,h,g),(h,g,h,g,h),…(g), (h), (g, h, g), (h, g, h), (g, h, g, h, g), (h, g, h, g, h), \dots(g),(h),(g,h,g),(h,g,h),(g,h,g,h,g),(h,g,h,g,h),…

which accordingly generate infinitely many distinct subgroups.

Tobias Schmude (Aug 21 2022 at 20:32):

Thanks! The nlab mentions another, more conceptual, way to see that Grp\mathrm{Grp}Grp does not have a subobject classifier:
First off, the trivial group is a zero object (both initial and terminal).
In a category with zero object, any subobject classified by a hypothetical subobject classifier turns out to be a kernel of its classifying map (see Prop 4.1 on the nlab).
This is a problem since not every subgroup is a kernel ("normal" subgroup).
