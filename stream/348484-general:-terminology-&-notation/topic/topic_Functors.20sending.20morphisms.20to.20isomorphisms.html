Topic: Functors sending morphisms to isomorphisms

Josselin Poiret (Jun 30 2023 at 15:52):

Is there a specific adjective for functors that send any morphism to an isomorphism? I want to be able to talk about it in the following way: the functor (x,y)↦F(x,y) (x,y) \mapsto F(x,y) (x,y)↦F(x,y) is ? in x x x.

Morgan Rogers (he/him) (Jun 30 2023 at 15:57):

As in FFF is a functor with two variables, and it's morphisms in the first variable which are sent to isomorphisms?

Josselin Poiret (Jun 30 2023 at 16:04):

yes, as in F(f,idy) F(f, id_y) F(f,idy​) is always an isomorphism

Josselin Poiret (Jun 30 2023 at 16:05):

it's in between being simply functorial and "parametric" (which would be that F(x,y)≃F(x′,y) F(x,y) \simeq F(x', y) F(x,y)≃F(x′,y) for all x,x′,y x,x',y x,x′,y)

John Baez (Jun 30 2023 at 22:58):

I've never heard of a name of functors that map all morphisms to isomorphisms (much less do so in one argument). They are extremely rare in my experience except when the target is a groupoid. So, I'm not surprised that I don't know of a name for such functors. I guess you can make one up. "Isomorphizing?" As in "the functor (x,y)→F(x,y)(x,y) \to F(x,y)(x,y)→F(x,y) is iromorphizing in xxx". But whatever name you use, you have to explain it.

Mike Shulman (Jun 30 2023 at 23:07):

Or "inverting" or "localizing", perhaps.

John Baez (Jun 30 2023 at 23:46):

By the way, I wouldn't make up a name unless I needed to say this > 3 times. It's pretty quick to say "FFF maps morphisms in the first variable to isomorphisms".
