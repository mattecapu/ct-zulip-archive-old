Jean-Baptiste Vienney said:

I'm interested by the "explanation available on request" on what is an homotopy fiber.

The short answer is: the homotopy fiber of a functor F:A→XF: A \to XF:A→X over a point xxx is the category of ways you can get xxx (up to isomorphism) by applying the functor FFF to some object of AAA. The "up to isomorphism" part is what makes it a homotopy fiber instead of an ordinary fiber.

In homotopy theory we are often interested in a map F:A→XF: A \to XF:A→X between topological spaces, and then the homotopy fiber of a point x∈Xx \in Xx∈X consists of points a∈Aa \in Aa∈A that don't need to have F(a)=xF(a) = xF(a)=x "on the nose": instead, it's enough to have a continuous path from F(a)F(a)F(a) to xxx.

John Baez (Apr 27 2023 at 23:50):

I hope you see that here we are using an analogy between

  • isomorphisms between objects in categories

and

  • paths between points in spaces.

This analogy is ultimately much more than an analogy, especially if we stick to categories that are just groupoids, or nnn-categories that are nnn-groupoids.
