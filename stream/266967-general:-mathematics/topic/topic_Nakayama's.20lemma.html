John Baez (Aug 30 2023 at 08:34):

There are many alternative formulations, some perhaps easier to remember than others. MathOverflow someone gave this nice mnemonic for it which happens to match what I actually need:

IM=M  ⟹  im=m I M = M \implies i m = m IM=M⟹im=m

What does this mean? It means:

Nakayama's Lemma. Let III be an ideal in a commutative ring RRR and let MMM be a finitely generated RRR-module. If IM=MIM=MIM=M then there exists i∈Ii \in Ii∈I such that im=mim = mim=m for all m∈Mm \in Mm∈M.

James Deikun (Aug 30 2023 at 11:51):

The ideal does have to be finitely generated to apply Nakayama's Lemma, indeed. But otherwise it's immediate, as:

  • an ideal inherits the structure of a module,
  • therefore there exists p∈Ip \in Ip∈I with pi=ipi = ipi=i for all i∈Ii \in Ii∈I,
  • in particular, p2=pp^2 = pp2=p,
  • pR⊆IpR \subseteq IpR⊆I since p∈Ip \in Ip∈I; I⊆pRI \subseteq pRI⊆pR since pI=IpI = IpI=I.
