Bruno Gavranovic (Mar 03 2023 at 19:30):

There's several ways to characterise a constant morphism c:B→Cc : B \to Cc:B→C in category theory. For instance, as a morphism factoring through a terminal object, or as a morphism with the property that for every other AAA, at most one morphism A→CA \to CA→C factors through it. In the category Set\mathbf{Set}Set this reduces to a constant function.

But I'm interested in the analogous property of dependent functions. That is, I want to understand when is a dependent function (a:A)→B(a)(a : A) \to B(a)(a:A)→B(a) constant, for some suitable meaning of this word. I come at this question from studying irrelevance/erasure in type theory, where we study maps like (0a:A)→B(a)(0 a : A) \to B(a)(0a:A)→B(a) - meaning the aaa can only influence the type of the codomain, but not the value.

Is something know about a possible characterisation of such function, analogous to characterisations of the non-dependent case?

Bruno Gavranovic (Mar 03 2023 at 19:31):

I should also add that for (0a:A)→B(a)(0 a : A) \to B(a)(0a:A)→B(a) I'm specifically interested in cases when B:A→SetB : A \to \mathbf{Set}B:A→Set itself is not constant - otherwise the whole situation trivialises.

Bruno Gavranovic (Mar 03 2023 at 19:36):

Bruno Gavranovic (Mar 03 2023 at 20:04):

Another way to think about this is that for the non-dependent case for all b,b′:Bb, b' : Bb,b′:B it holds that c(b)=c(b′)c(b) = c(b')c(b)=c(b′).
But generalising this to the dependent case grounds to a halt - the results c(b):B(b)c(b) : B(b)c(b):B(b) and c(b′):B(b′)c(b') : B(b')c(b′):B(b′) are of different types!

It looks like we might need to use some notion of heterogeneous equality. However, on nLab the part about its categorical semantics is unfortunately empty :face_with_diagonal_mouth:

Josselin Poiret (Mar 04 2023 at 09:30):

you might want to look into shape-irrelevance: basically, if you want to be able to form (0 a : A) → B a, B must already be shape-irrelevant in its argument. This means that for two unrelated inputs x and y, B x and B y are related, and for types in the universe this roughly means that they support some heterogeneous equality. Once you have that heterogeneous equality, you can state what being constant is!

Josselin Poiret (Mar 04 2023 at 09:31):

for some references, you can look into paramdtt (https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3110276) or Andreas Nuyts' thesis in general.

Josselin Poiret (Mar 04 2023 at 09:33):

see also https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.04383 for a model
