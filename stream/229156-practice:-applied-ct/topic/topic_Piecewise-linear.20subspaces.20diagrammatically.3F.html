Guillaume Boisseau (Jun 04 2021 at 13:40):

Le big summary of things so far

Background

We have a sound and complete diagrammatic theory of convex polyhedra (that I will call simply "polyhedra") here: https://arxiv.org/abs/2105.10946 . Polyhedron here also includes polyhedral-like shapes that go to infinity, for example the top-right quadrant x,y≥0x,y \geq 0x,y≥0. Terms in this theory are diagrams made from some basic generators, see there. An n→mn \to mn→m diagram in this language is interpreted as a polyhedron in Rn+m\mathbb{R}^{n+m}Rn+m.

The theory

What we're trying to do is extend this to arbitrary unions of polyhedra. To do that, we add to our language a union operator on diagrams of the same type, and some axioms. So far I think we want:

  • union distributes over composition and tensor

  • union and the intersection already present in the language form a distributive
    lattice

  • x≥0∪x≤0=⊤x \geq 0 \cup x \leq 0 = \topx≥0∪x≤0=⊤ (aka totality)

The semantics functor (call it SSS) now maps a term in this language to a union of polyhedra in Rn+m\mathbb{R}^{n+m}Rn+m. I believe the axioms mentioned above are enough to get completeness, namely that when S(X)⊂S(Y)S(X) \subset S(Y)S(X)⊂S(Y) then X⊂YX \subset YX⊂Y is provable in the theory.

Normal form

Single polyhedra (i.e. terms that don't feature the union operator) have a normal form, described in the paper above. The normal form expresses a polyhedron as the intersection of a bunch of half-spaces, where a half-space is H+:={x∣H(x)≥0}H^+ := \{ x | H(x) \geq 0 \}H+:={x∣H(x)≥0} for some nonzero affine function HHH. I sometimes write HHH or H0H^0H0 for the hyperplane {x∣H(x)=0}\{ x | H(x) = 0 \}{x∣H(x)=0}, and tend to call HHH itself a hyperplane too.

Given a general term XXX, we can move all the unions to the top-level to get an explicit union of diagrams without unions aka polyhedra. We then normalize all those subpolyhedra. We end up with a form where XXX is the union of intersections of half-spaces. Moreover, A=(A∩H+)∪(A∩H−)A = (A \cap H^+) \cup (A \cap H^-)A=(A∩H+)∪(A∩H−) is provable, so we can add some half-spaces to a given subpolyhedron, so that in the end XXX is a union of intersections of half-spaces, and each term in the union mentions the same set of hyperplanes.

Trying to prove completeness

Take XXX and YYY such that S(X)⊂S(Y)S(X) \subset S(Y)S(X)⊂S(Y). First normalize both X and Y. From the lattice axioms A∪B⊂Y  ⟺  A⊂Y∧B⊂YA \cup B \subset Y \iff A \subset Y \land B \subset YA∪B⊂Y⟺A⊂Y∧B⊂Y, so wlog we can assume that XXX doesn't use the union operator, i.e. is a single polyhedron.

Now X⊂Y  ⟺  X=X∩YX \subset Y \iff X = X \cap YX⊂Y⟺X=X∩Y. So for completeness it suffices to be able to prove X=YX = YX=Y when XXX is a single polyhedron and S(Y)=S(X)S(Y) = S(X)S(Y)=S(X) is convex. The direction Y⊂XY \subset XY⊂X reduces to inclusions between single polyhedra, which we can prove by completeness of the theory of single polyhedra. So remains only the case X⊂YX \subset YX⊂Y where XXX is a single polyhedron and S(Y)S(Y)S(Y) is convex.

The final reduction, which might not be the right approach, is as follows. Take all the hyperplanes in the normal form of YYY and consider all the possible intersections of the corresponding half-spaces. Call each of those a cell. From our axioms we can show that the union of all cells is the whole space. From this and lattice axioms we get X⊂Y  ⟺  ∀c.X∩c⊂Y∩cX \subset Y \iff \forall c. X \cap c \subset Y \cap cX⊂Y⟺∀c.X∩c⊂Y∩c where ccc ranges over the cells. Remember that YYY is written as a union of some of those cells. I'm hoping this makes it easy to express Y∩cY \cap cY∩c as a single polyhedron, which would entail completeness.
