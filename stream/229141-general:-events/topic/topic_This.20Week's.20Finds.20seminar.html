John Baez (Sep 16 2022 at 08:32):

The first lecture will be on Young diagrams and representations of the classical groups:

Young diagrams

Young diagrams are combinatorial structures that show up in a myriad of applications. Among other things, they classify conjugacy classes in the symmetric groups SnS_nSn​, irreducible representations of SnS_nSn​, irreducible representations of the groups SL(n)\mathrm{SL}(n)SL(n) over any field of characteristic zero, and irreducible unitary representations of the groups SU(n)\mathrm{SU}(n)SU(n).

Yes, the direct sum is the coproduct! You can cook up dream up morphisms ρ→ρ⊕π\rho \to \rho \oplus \piρ→ρ⊕π and π→ρ⊕π\pi \to \rho \oplus \piπ→ρ⊕π and check that they have the universal property of the coproduct.

Actually all this is vastly easier if you first check that the direct sum of vector spaces is the coproduct in Vect\mathsf{Vect}Vect. Then the other structure sort of goes along for the ride. Trying to check everything all at once is bit heavy.

John Baez (Sep 16 2022 at 16:44):

Another cool thing to check is that the direct sum of two vector spaces is also their product in Vect\mathsf{Vect}Vect! And this is true for group representations, too: the direct sum of two representations is both a product and coproduct.

John Baez (Sep 16 2022 at 16:47):

I would say that one of the great charms of linear algebra is that the product of two objects is also the coproduct. This gives rise to the theory of matrices.
